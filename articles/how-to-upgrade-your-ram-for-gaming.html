How to Upgrade Your RAM for Gaming

By TechGameHQ Staff May 2026 7 min read Tech Guide

A RAM upgrade is one of the cheapest and most impactful things you can do for your PC. More RAM means fewer stutters, smoother multitasking, and better performance in modern games — and it takes under 10 minutes to install.

How Much RAM Do You Actually Need?

Not all RAM upgrades are equal. The difference between 8GB and 16GB is massive for gaming. The jump from 16GB to 32GB is noticeable but not essential for most players.

AmountGaming PerformanceBest For
8GBStruggling in 2026Very old or casual games only
16GBSweet spotMost gamers — handles any modern game
32GBFuture-proofHeavy multitasking, streaming while gaming
64GB+Overkill for gamingVideo editing, 3D rendering, workstations

If you have 8GB, upgrading to 16GB will be one of the most noticeable improvements you can make. If you already have 16GB and games feel smooth, there's no rush to upgrade.

DDR4 vs DDR5 — Which Do You Have?

Your motherboard determines which type of RAM you can use — you cannot mix DDR4 and DDR5, and they are not interchangeable.

  • DDR4 — Most PCs built before 2022. Still performs great for gaming.
  • DDR5 — Newer platforms (Intel 12th gen+, AMD Ryzen 7000+). Faster, but more expensive.
💡 How to Check What You Have Download CPU-Z (free), open it, and go to the Memory tab. It will show you DDR4 or DDR5, your current speed, and how many slots are used.

How to Choose Compatible RAM

Before buying, you need to know three things:

  1. DDR generation — DDR4 or DDR5 (checked above)
  2. Maximum speed your motherboard supports — check your motherboard's spec sheet or manufacturer website
  3. Number of free slots — most motherboards have 2 or 4 slots. If all slots are full, you'll need to replace existing sticks.

For best performance, always buy RAM in matched pairs (a kit of 2 sticks). This enables dual-channel mode, which gives a noticeable performance boost over a single stick of the same total capacity.

Step-by-Step: Installing RAM

Step 1

Power off and unplug your PC

Shut down completely and unplug from the wall. Press the power button once after unplugging to discharge any remaining electricity.

Step 2

Open the case and locate the RAM slots

RAM slots are the long, thin slots next to the CPU. Most motherboards have 2 or 4. They have small plastic clips at each end.

Step 3

Remove existing RAM (if replacing)

Press down on both clips simultaneously — the stick will pop up at an angle. Pull it out gently. Handle RAM by the edges, never touch the gold contacts.

Step 4

Insert the new RAM

Check the notch on the RAM stick aligns with the slot — it only goes in one way. Press firmly and evenly on both ends until both clips click into place. If it doesn't click, don't force it — recheck the alignment.

Step 5

Enable XMP / EXPO in BIOS

Boot your PC and enter the BIOS (usually Del or F2 on startup). Find the XMP (Intel) or EXPO (AMD) setting and enable it. This runs your RAM at its advertised speed instead of the default slower speed. Save and restart.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid Don't mix RAM from different kits — even the same brand and speed can cause instability. Don't skip enabling XMP/EXPO — without it, your fast RAM runs at a slower default speed. Don't install RAM while the PC is plugged in.

How to Verify the Upgrade Worked

After booting, right-click the Start menu and open Task Manager. Go to the Performance tab and click Memory. You should see the new total amount and, importantly, the speed should match your RAM's rated speed (if you enabled XMP/EXPO).

Recommended RAM Kits

For most gamers, a 16GB DDR4 3200MHz kit is the best value upgrade in 2026. If you're on a newer DDR5 platform, a 32GB DDR5 kit is worth considering.

The Verdict

If you're on 8GB, upgrade to 16GB — it's the single best value improvement you can make right now. Install it in dual-channel, enable XMP/EXPO, and you'll notice the difference immediately in load times, stutter reduction, and multitasking.

The whole process takes under 10 minutes and costs far less than any GPU or CPU upgrade. It's one of those rare things in PC gaming where the effort-to-reward ratio is essentially perfect.
