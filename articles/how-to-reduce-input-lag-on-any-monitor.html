How to Reduce Input Lag on Any Monitor

By Fernando Oliveira May 2026 10 min read Tech

Input lag is the delay between moving your mouse or pressing a key and seeing the result on screen. Even a few milliseconds can make games feel sluggish. Here's how to minimize it — mostly for free. For a complete hardware picture, also check out the best budget gaming monitors if you're considering a display upgrade, and our guide to getting better at FPS games where settings optimization is covered alongside skill improvement.

What Causes Input Lag?

Input lag comes from multiple sources stacking up: your monitor's response time, V-Sync adding delay, Windows processing, and your GPU render queue. The good news is most of these can be reduced through settings alone. If your system still feels sluggish after these fixes, it might be thermal throttling rather than input lag — check our guide on monitoring your PC temperature to rule that out.

Fix 1: Turn Off V-Sync

Free — Do This First

Disable V-Sync In-Game and in GPU Settings

V-Sync synchronizes your FPS to your monitor refresh rate, which sounds good but adds 1-3 frames of input lag. Turn it off in every game's settings. Also disable it in your GPU control panel (NVIDIA Control Panel or AMD Radeon Software) for a global override.

If you get screen tearing without V-Sync, use G-Sync or FreeSync instead — they eliminate tearing without the input lag penalty.

Fix 2: Enable Game Mode in Windows

Free

Windows Game Mode Reduces Background Interference

Press Windows + I → Gaming → Game Mode → Toggle ON. This prioritizes your game for system resources and reduces background process interference, which can shave off a few ms of input lag. For a broader set of Windows optimizations, our guide on optimizing Windows 11 for gaming covers additional tweaks that work alongside this.

Fix 3: Use Your Monitor's Game Mode

Monitor Setting

Enable Game Mode or Low Input Lag Mode on Your Monitor

Most gaming monitors have a dedicated "Game Mode" or "Low Input Lag" setting in their OSD menu (the buttons on the side or bottom of your monitor). This disables image processing features that add delay. It might make the image look slightly less vibrant but the responsiveness improvement is real.

Fix 4: Set Your Monitor to Its Maximum Refresh Rate

Free — Often Missed

Check Your Refresh Rate in Windows Settings

Many people have a 144Hz monitor running at 60Hz because Windows defaulted to the lower rate. Right-click your desktop → Display Settings → Advanced Display → choose the highest refresh rate available. This alone can make games feel dramatically smoother.

💡 Use DisplayPort Not HDMI If your monitor supports both, use DisplayPort over HDMI. DisplayPort handles high refresh rates more reliably and can reduce input lag compared to some HDMI implementations. Use HDMI 2.1 only if you need it for a console.

Fix 5: Limit FPS Slightly Below Your Refresh Rate

Free — Advanced

Cap FPS at Refresh Rate Minus 3-4 Frames

If your monitor is 144Hz, cap your FPS at 141. This keeps your GPU from building up a render queue (which adds lag) while staying close to your maximum refresh rate. Use RTSS (RivaTuner Statistics Server, comes with MSI Afterburner) to set a precise FPS cap.

Fix 6: Turn Off Unnecessary Monitor Features

In your monitor's OSD menu, disable these features that add processing delay:

  • HDR — adds significant processing lag unless you have a true HDR monitor
  • Dynamic Contrast — turn off
  • Motion Blur Reduction / ULMB — can add lag on some monitors
  • Overdrive/Response Time — set to Medium, not Maximum (causes overshoot)

Fix 7: Optimize Your GPU Driver Settings

Free — NVIDIA Users

Enable NVIDIA Reflex (or Low Latency Mode)

NVIDIA Reflex is supported in Valorant, Fortnite, Apex Legends, and many other competitive titles. It reduces the render queue depth, which is one of the primary sources of GPU-side input lag. Enable it in your game's settings (look for "NVIDIA Reflex" or "Reflex Low Latency"). The "Enabled + Boost" setting is often the best choice for most systems.

If your game doesn't support Reflex, go to NVIDIA Control Panel → Manage 3D Settings → Low Latency Mode → set to "Ultra". This does the same job for unsupported titles, at the cost of slightly lower maximum FPS.

Free — AMD Users

Enable AMD Anti-Lag

AMD's equivalent to NVIDIA Reflex is Anti-Lag and Anti-Lag+. Enable it in AMD Software: Adrenalin Edition under the Gaming tab for your game. Anti-Lag+ works at the driver level and is compatible with a wider range of titles. Combined with turning off V-Sync, this can reduce total system latency by 10–30ms depending on your setup.

How to Measure Your Input Lag

Feeling the improvement is useful, but measuring it gives you hard numbers to compare. Use RTSS (RivaTuner Statistics Server) combined with the FrameTime view to see your render pipeline latency. For monitor-specific lag, tools like NVIDIA FrameView report PC-side latency. The most accurate consumer tool is a high-speed camera filming your screen and mouse simultaneously, but that's overkill for most people — RTSS data is more than sufficient for tuning.

If you want an accessible check: play a fast-paced game and pay attention to how the game "feels" with V-Sync off vs. on. The difference with V-Sync enabled is typically noticeable as a slight mushiness in mouse movement. If you can feel that difference, you can feel the other improvements too.

The Role of Your Monitor Panel Type

Not all monitors process images at the same speed. Panel type has a significant effect on response time and perceived input lag:

  • TN panels: Fastest response times (1ms GtG), lowest input lag. Colors and viewing angles are poor. Still popular for competitive esports at 240Hz+.
  • IPS panels: Good colors, acceptable response times (1–4ms GtG on fast gaming panels). The standard choice for most gamers — good balance of quality and responsiveness.
  • VA panels: Best contrast ratio, slowest response on dark transitions ("black smearing"). Good for movies, suboptimal for fast-paced FPS games.
  • OLED panels: Near-instant response times, stunning contrast. Expensive, with burn-in risk if displaying static HUD elements for extended hours. Increasingly viable as prices drop.

If you're in the market for a new display, check the best budget gaming monitors under $200 — there are solid 144Hz IPS options that deliver genuinely low input lag without breaking the bank. You might also want to look into whether setting up dual monitors makes sense for your workflow.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Enabling V-Sync and blaming the hardware: V-Sync is the single biggest source of added input lag on most systems. It's almost always the first thing to disable, and many gamers forget they enabled it months ago in a game's settings.
  • Running HDR on a budget monitor: Software HDR on monitors without real local dimming adds significant image processing delay and doesn't improve image quality. Disable it.
  • Mistaking FPS for refresh rate: Your monitor can only display as many frames as its refresh rate allows. If your monitor is 60Hz, getting 300 FPS in-game does nothing for smoothness — but it does reduce input lag because the GPU renders a more recent frame right before your monitor refreshes. Cap FPS to 2–3x your refresh rate for the best balance.
  • Ignoring in-game resolution scale: Rendering at 110% resolution scale for sharper images adds significant GPU processing time and increases input lag. Always render at 100% for the best responsiveness.

Advanced Tips

USB Polling Rate for Your Mouse

Most gaming mice poll at 1000Hz, meaning they send position data to your PC 1000 times per second (every 1ms). Some newer mice support 4000Hz or 8000Hz polling, which reduces the average latency from mouse movement to display update. This is a micro-optimization — you'd need to measure it to notice it — but for ultra-competitive players, it's available. Check your mouse software for polling rate settings.

Disable Xbox Game Bar

Xbox Game Bar (Windows + G) runs as an overlay and can add input lag by consuming system resources in the background. Disable it: Settings → Gaming → Xbox Game Bar → toggle off. Also disable "Game DVR" background recording if enabled, as continuous recording has a measurable impact on both performance and input lag.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a faster SSD reduce input lag?

No. Storage speed affects load times, not input lag. Input lag is entirely in the render pipeline — CPU, GPU, monitor, and OS. An NVMe SSD will make your game load faster and reduce texture pop-in in open-world games, but it has no effect on how responsive your controls feel in-game.

Is G-Sync worth it for reducing input lag?

G-Sync (NVIDIA) and FreeSync (AMD) are variable refresh rate technologies that sync your monitor's refresh rate to your GPU's current frame output. They eliminate screen tearing without the input lag penalty of V-Sync. For most gamers, FreeSync monitors paired with an AMD or NVIDIA GPU (G-Sync Compatible mode) offer the best of both worlds — no tearing, minimal added lag. Pure G-Sync modules have slightly better performance but cost significantly more.

My game feels laggy even at 144Hz — why?

A high refresh rate monitor doesn't automatically mean low input lag. Check: V-Sync may be enabled, your GPU may be rendering below 144 FPS (meaning your monitor can't display 144 distinct frames per second), or monitor features like Super Resolution, HDR, or black equalization may be adding processing delay. Work through the fixes in this article systematically and you should identify the culprit.

Quick Checklist

Do these right now and your games will feel noticeably more responsive:

  1. Turn off V-Sync in-game and GPU settings
  2. Enable Game Mode in Windows
  3. Enable Game Mode on your monitor
  4. Confirm your monitor is running at max refresh rate
  5. Switch to DisplayPort if you're using HDMI
  6. Cap FPS just below your refresh rate
