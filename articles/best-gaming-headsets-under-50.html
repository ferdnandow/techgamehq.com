What to Look for Before You Buy

Budget headsets vary wildly in actual quality. The marketing on cheap gaming peripherals is notoriously misleading — "7.1 surround" on a $30 headset means software processing, not actual physical drivers. Here's what to evaluate before pulling the trigger:

  • Driver size: 40mm drivers are standard on budget headsets. 50mm drivers (like on the EKSA E1000 and Razer BlackShark V2 X) produce richer, wider sound. Bigger isn't always better, but 50mm generally handles bass and soundstage better at budget price points.
  • Connection type: USB headsets get their audio processed on the headset's own DAC/amp — which can be good or bad depending on the hardware. 3.5mm headsets use your motherboard or controller's audio. If your motherboard has decent onboard audio, 3.5mm is often cleaner. USB is better if your onboard audio is noisy.
  • Mic type: Fixed (non-detachable) mics are standard at this price. Flip-to-mute is more convenient than inline mute buttons — you can mute quickly without fumbling. Cardioid mics reject more side and background noise than omnidirectional mics.
  • Comfort for long sessions: Weight matters a lot for 4+ hour sessions. The Logitech G335 at 240g is noticeably lighter than most competitors. Velour earpads breathe better than leatherette — important in warm rooms.
  • Platform compatibility: If you game on both PC and console, check the connection. USB headsets don't work on PS5/Xbox without an adapter. A 3.5mm headset works universally across PC, PS5, Xbox, and Switch.

Headset vs Earbuds: Which Is Right for You?

A full gaming headset isn't the only option at this price. A good pair of gaming earbuds under $50 can deliver comparable audio in a much more portable package. Earbuds are better for mobile gaming, hot climates, and people who don't want something on their head for hours. Headsets are better for immersive single-player games, spatial audio in competitive shooters, and people who prefer the weight and seal of over-ear cups.

If you game primarily on PC and care about positional audio in games like CS2, Valorant, or Warzone, over-ear headsets with large 50mm drivers provide a wider soundstage than most earbuds at this price. For mobile and casual gaming, earbuds make more sense.

Common Mistakes When Buying Budget Gaming Headsets

  • Trusting "7.1 surround" marketing: Virtual 7.1 surround is software simulated, and on budget headsets, it often sounds worse than plain stereo — adding artificial echo and reducing clarity. Test with surround off first and only enable it if it genuinely improves your experience.
  • Ignoring mic quality: You'll be judged by your teammates based on mic quality. The Razer BlackShark V2 X has a noticeably better mic than budget competitors. If you play team games heavily, prioritize mic quality over headset sound.
  • Buying wireless at this price: Wireless gaming headsets under $50 exist but generally have poor battery life, higher latency, and weaker audio than their wired equivalents. Save up for wireless, or go wired at this budget.
  • Not checking earcup size: Some budget headsets have small earcups that sit on your ears rather than around them. This gets uncomfortable quickly. Look for "over-ear" not "on-ear" if you plan to wear them for 3+ hour sessions.

Pro Tips for Getting the Best Sound from Your Headset

Pro Tip Windows Sonic for Headphones (free, built into Windows 11) provides decent spatial audio for gaming without requiring any software install. Enable it by right-clicking the speaker icon in the taskbar — it works with any headset and noticeably improves directional awareness in FPS games.
  • Use Equalizer APO: This free Windows equalizer lets you fine-tune your headset's sound profile. Budget headsets often have overpowered bass — reducing 200Hz and boosting 2–5kHz range improves voice clarity and footstep detection in competitive games.
  • Position the mic correctly: Budget mics are most sensitive at the tip. Position the mic about 2cm from the corner of your mouth, not directly in front — this reduces "plosive" sounds (p and b sounds causing wind noise).
  • Clean the earpads regularly: PU leather earpads accumulate sweat and oils that degrade the material. Wipe them down monthly with a slightly damp cloth. This extends their life significantly.
  • Check your in-game audio settings: Many games have audio settings that are poorly optimized by default. In CS2 and Valorant, enabling HRTF (head-related transfer function) dramatically improves positional audio without any third-party software. Combine this with your headset for a noticeable competitive edge.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a gaming headset better than regular headphones for gaming?

Not always. Regular headphones (like the Audio-Technica ATH-M20x or M40x) often have better sound quality than gaming headsets at the same price. The trade-off is that regular headphones don't have built-in mics. If audio quality is your priority, consider a good pair of stereo headphones plus a separate clip-on or USB mic — you'll likely get better sound for the same $50 budget.

Does the cable length matter for a gaming headset?

Yes, especially for desktop setups. A cable that's too short forces you to sit close to your PC or use extension cables. Most gaming headsets come with 1.5–2m cables, which is sufficient for most desk setups. USB headsets occasionally come with 1.8m cables that may not reach the front of some tower PCs — check before buying.

How do I get better at FPS games using audio?

Positional audio is a major competitive advantage. Practice listening for footstep direction and distance, gunshot echoes, and reload sounds. Enable HRTF or spatial audio in your game settings. A headset with large 50mm drivers and a wide soundstage (like the Razer BlackShark V2 X) helps significantly. For more tips, read our guide on how to get better at FPS games.
