How Gaming-Specific Threats Work (And How to Avoid Them)

Gamers are targeted by specific types of malware that general antivirus guides often don't address. Understanding what these threats look like helps you avoid them — and helps you understand why the antivirus recommendations above are specifically suited to the gaming context.

Credential-stealing malware targets gaming account login data. These programs install quietly (often bundled with cracked games or "free DLC" downloads) and monitor clipboard content and browser saved passwords for Steam, Battle.net, Riot Games, and Epic Games credentials. Once stolen, accounts with valuable inventories or game libraries are sold or ransomed. Windows Defender and Malwarebytes both catch most of these — but the best defense is never downloading software from unofficial sources.

Cryptominers disguise themselves as game mods, trainers, or utility software. They run silently in the background and use your GPU or CPU to mine cryptocurrency, causing your games to run poorly and your hardware to run hot. The symptom is unusually high GPU/CPU usage even when you're not doing much. MSI Afterburner's overlay will surface this immediately — which is another reason having performance monitoring tools installed is valuable beyond just optimization. Our guide on how to monitor your PC temperature also helps you spot unusual thermal activity that may indicate a cryptominer.

Fake game launchers impersonate legitimate launchers (Steam, Epic, Battle.net) and harvest login credentials. They're typically distributed through unofficial game download sites, Discord servers, and phishing emails. The easiest defense is always downloading launchers from official websites and checking the URL carefully before entering credentials.

Keeping your system clean also extends to regular maintenance. Our guide on how to clean your PC covers both physical cleaning (dust buildup reduces cooling efficiency) and software cleanup — removing junk files and unnecessary startup programs that slow your system down.

Pro Tip Enable Windows Defender's "Controlled Folder Access" feature — it prevents unauthorized programs from modifying files in protected folders like Documents, Desktop, and Pictures. This is especially useful for protecting game save files from ransomware. Find it in Windows Security > Virus & Threat Protection > Ransomware Protection.

Tips to Stay Protected Without Hurting Gaming Performance

Schedule scans for off-peak hours. Even lightweight antivirus scans use some CPU and disk I/O. In Windows Defender, you can schedule full scans for times when you're not gaming — 3 AM on a Tuesday, for instance. Go to Task Scheduler > Microsoft > Windows > Windows Defender to set this up.

Add game folders to exclusion lists carefully. Many gaming guides suggest adding your entire Steam or game folders to antivirus exclusion lists to prevent false positives. This is generally fine for established games, but don't add exclusions blindly — it prevents Defender from scanning game files for malware. Only exclude folders if you're experiencing confirmed false positive detections.

Keep everything updated. Antivirus software is only as good as its latest signature definitions. Windows Defender updates automatically with Windows Update, but make sure automatic updates aren't disabled on your system. Outdated definitions can miss new threats, especially novel malware variants targeting fresh game releases. Our guide on how to speed up your PC for gaming includes tips on keeping your system lean and up-to-date without unnecessary bloat.

Use two-factor authentication on all gaming accounts. No antivirus catches 100% of threats, and credential theft is increasingly common. Enabling 2FA on Steam, Epic Games, Battle.net, and Riot accounts means stolen passwords alone aren't enough to compromise your account. This is the single most important account security step you can take — and it's completely free.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Windows Defender really good enough, or is that just what Microsoft says?

Independent testing confirms it. AV-TEST, AV-Comparatives, and SE Labs — the three most respected independent antivirus testing organizations — consistently rate Windows Defender in their top tier for protection, performance, and usability. In the most recent AV-TEST evaluations, Defender scored 6/6 in protection and 6/6 in performance, matching products costing $50-$80 per year. The days of Defender being a bare-minimum option are long gone — it's genuinely competitive in 2026.

Will running antivirus cause my games to stutter or drop frames?

With Windows Defender, the answer is effectively no in normal use. Defender uses real-time scanning that monitors file access as it happens, which has a measurable but extremely small overhead — typically less than 1% CPU impact during gaming. The exception is if Defender triggers a full scan while you're mid-game, which can cause brief CPU spikes. Scheduling full scans for off-hours prevents this entirely. Malwarebytes Free doesn't run in the background at all (free version is manual scan only), so it has zero gaming impact.

Do I need antivirus if I only play games on Steam and never download anything else?

Steam itself is generally safe, but it's not the only attack vector. Malicious browser extensions, phishing websites that mimic game login pages, and social engineering through Discord and gaming communities are all real threats that don't involve downloading executable files. Windows Defender provides protection against all of these scenarios — and since it's already installed and uses minimal resources, there's no reason not to keep it active. Pair it with a secure browser like Brave for comprehensive protection without spending anything.
