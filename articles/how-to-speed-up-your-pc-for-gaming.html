How to Speed Up Your PC for Gaming (Free Methods That Actually Work)

By Fernando Oliveira May 2026 13 min read Tech

Your PC is stuttering, your FPS is dropping, and you're getting destroyed in-game because of lag. Before you spend money on new hardware, try these free optimizations first — most gamers see a noticeable improvement within 30 minutes.

1. Update Your GPU Drivers

Outdated GPU drivers are one of the most common causes of poor gaming performance. Both NVIDIA and AMD release driver updates regularly that include performance improvements for new games.

For NVIDIA users

Download GeForce Experience

Go to nvidia.com/geforce/geforce-experience, download and install the app, then click "Drivers" and hit "Check for Updates". Install the latest driver and restart your PC.

For AMD users

Use AMD Adrenalin Software

Download AMD Radeon Software from amd.com/support. Open it, go to the "Home" tab, and check for driver updates. One click installs everything automatically.

💡 Pro Tip Always do a clean install when updating GPU drivers. In the NVIDIA installer, choose "Custom Installation" and check "Perform a clean installation" to remove old driver files that can cause conflicts.

2. Disable Background Apps and Startup Programs

Every app running in the background is stealing RAM and CPU resources from your game. Here's how to stop them:

  1. Press Ctrl + Shift + Esc to open Task Manager
  2. Click the Startup tab
  3. Right-click any app you don't need at startup and select Disable
  4. Common culprits: Discord, Spotify, OneDrive, Teams, Skype, Steam (if you launch it manually)

You can also press Windows + R, type msconfig, and under the "Services" tab, check "Hide all Microsoft services" then disable anything you don't recognize.

3. Enable Game Mode in Windows

Windows 10 and 11 have a built-in Game Mode that prioritizes your game for CPU and GPU resources. It's free and takes 10 seconds to enable.

  1. Press Windows + I to open Settings
  2. Go to Gaming → Game Mode
  3. Toggle Game Mode ON
💡 Also do this While you're in Gaming settings, go to "Graphics" and add your game manually. Set it to "High Performance" to ensure your dedicated GPU is always used instead of integrated graphics.

4. Adjust Your Power Plan

If your PC is set to "Balanced" or "Power Saver" mode, Windows is throttling your CPU to save electricity. Switch to High Performance:

  1. Press Windows + R, type powercfg.cpl and press Enter
  2. Select High Performance (click "Show additional plans" if you don't see it)
  3. Close the window

On laptops, plug in your charger and set the battery mode to "Best Performance" in the taskbar battery icon.

5. Clear Your Temp Files and Disk Space

Windows accumulates junk files over time that can slow down your whole system, including games. Clean them out:

Quick method

Use Disk Cleanup

Press Windows + R, type cleanmgr, select your C: drive, check all boxes including "Temporary files" and "Recycle Bin", then click OK and Delete Files.

Even faster

Delete Temp folder manually

Press Windows + R, type %temp%, press Enter. Select all files (Ctrl+A) and delete them. Skip any files that say they're in use.

6. Lower In-Game Settings Strategically

Not all graphics settings impact performance equally. Here's what to lower first:

  • Anti-Aliasing — huge performance cost, barely noticeable at 1080p. Set to OFF or FXAA only.
  • Shadows — set to Medium instead of Ultra. Huge FPS gain.
  • Ambient Occlusion — turn off completely. Barely visible difference.
  • Motion Blur — turn off. Most gamers hate it anyway.
  • V-Sync — turn off if you don't have screen tearing issues. Adds input lag.
💡 Keep these HIGH Texture Quality and Resolution Scale have the biggest visual impact. Keep them as high as your FPS allows.

7. Install MSI Afterburner (Free)

MSI Afterburner is the best free tool for monitoring and boosting GPU performance. It works with all GPU brands, not just MSI.

  • Download free at msi.com/Landing/afterburner
  • Use it to monitor your GPU/CPU temps and FPS in-game
  • You can also overclock your GPU for free performance gains (only if temps are safe)
⚠️ Warning Only overclock if your GPU temperatures are below 85°C under load. Monitor temperatures before touching any OC settings. When in doubt, just use it for monitoring.

8. Check for Malware

Malware running in the background can silently destroy your PC performance. Run a free scan with Windows Defender:

  1. Press Windows + S and search for Windows Security
  2. Click Virus & threat protection
  3. Click Quick scan

For a deeper scan, use Malwarebytes Free — the free version is excellent for one-time scans.

Common Mistakes That Hurt PC Gaming Performance

Most performance issues aren't caused by bad hardware — they're caused by avoidable configuration mistakes. Here are the most common ones we see:

  • Running the game off an HDD instead of an SSD — load times and texture streaming are dramatically slower on spinning drives. If you only have one SSD, move your most-played games there first. Read our SSD vs HDD for gaming breakdown for a full comparison.
  • Running games in windowed mode — windowed mode adds a compositor layer that costs performance. Always use fullscreen or borderless windowed for gaming.
  • Ignoring Windows updates for months — critical driver updates and security patches are delivered through Windows Update. Staying 6+ months behind can cause instability and compatibility issues with new games.
  • Letting your storage get above 90% full — Windows needs free space for virtual memory and temporary files. A nearly-full drive causes system-wide slowdowns. Keep at least 15% of your main drive free.
  • Not restarting the PC for days — Windows accumulates memory leaks and background processes over long uptimes. A fresh restart before a gaming session clears all of that.

Advanced Optimization: Next Level Tweaks

You've done the basics. Here's what to do next if you want to push performance further without spending money.

Enable XMP/EXPO in BIOS for RAM speed

Most RAM ships at a slower speed than it's rated for. To unlock your RAM's full speed, enter your BIOS (press Delete or F2 at boot), find the XMP (Intel) or EXPO (AMD) setting, and enable it. This sets your RAM to run at its advertised speed — often 3200MHz or faster — instead of the default 2133MHz. It's one of the easiest free performance boosts for gaming and takes two minutes. If you're wondering whether to upgrade your RAM for gaming, first try enabling XMP to get full value from what you have.

Set games to High Priority in Task Manager

While a game is running, press Ctrl + Shift + Esc, find the game's process, right-click it, and set Priority to High. This tells Windows to give the game more CPU scheduling priority. Note: this resets when you close and reopen the game, so you'd need to do it each session — or use a tool like Process Lasso to automate it.

Defragment your HDD (not SSD)

If games are stored on a traditional hard drive, defragmentation reorganizes fragmented files so they load faster. Search for "Defragment and Optimize Drives" in Windows. Run this on HDDs only — SSDs should use "Optimize" (TRIM) instead, which Windows handles automatically.

Check for and remove bloatware

New PCs — especially from OEMs like Dell, HP, and Lenovo — often ship with pre-installed software that runs in the background. Go to Settings → Apps → Installed apps and uninstall anything you don't recognize or need: trial antivirus software, promotional apps, manufacturer utilities you never use. This frees up RAM, CPU, and disk resources immediately.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much RAM do I actually need for gaming in 2026?

16GB is the current minimum for modern gaming — most games list it as recommended. 32GB is becoming the comfortable standard for gaming while running Discord, a browser, and OBS simultaneously. If you're on 8GB, upgrading is likely the single most impactful hardware change you can make right now. Games like Hogwarts Legacy and newer AAA titles regularly use 12–15GB of RAM during play.

Do free "PC booster" apps actually work?

No. Apps like Advanced SystemCare, ioBit, and similar "optimizers" are borderline scam software. They show fake issues, delete files Windows uses, and often bundle adware or worse. Every legitimate optimization they claim to do, you can do yourself in Windows for free using the steps in this guide. Save your money and avoid them entirely.

My PC was fast when I bought it — why is it slow now?

This almost always comes down to software accumulation over time: startup programs, background apps, malware, disk clutter, and outdated drivers all compound. A fresh Windows install every 2–3 years dramatically restores performance, but before going that far, work through every step in this guide — most people recover significant performance without needing to reinstall.

Final Thoughts

Before spending any money, go through this list top to bottom. Most gamers see 10-30% FPS improvements just from updating drivers, disabling startup apps, and switching to High Performance power mode.

If you've done everything here and your PC is still struggling, the next step is hardware — but that's a topic for another guide. For now, these free tweaks should make a real difference.
