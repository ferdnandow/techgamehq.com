How to Choose PC Parts on a Budget

By Fernando Oliveira May 2026 7 min read Tech Guide

Building a gaming PC on a budget isn’t about buying the cheapest parts — it’s about knowing where to spend and where to save. Spend wrong and you end up with a bottlenecked build that underperforms. Spend right and you get 80% of the performance for 50% of the price. Once your build is complete, check our guides on upgrading your RAM for gaming and SSD vs HDD for gaming to fine-tune your storage and memory configuration.

Budget Allocation: Where Your Money Should Go

Most people building on a budget make the same mistake: they spread money evenly across all parts. Don’t. The GPU and CPU are what determine gaming performance. Everything else supports them.

Part-by-Part Buying Guide

GPU — Spend the most here
Graphics Card

The GPU is the single most important component for gaming. In 2026, the sweet spot for budget gaming is the RX 7600 (AMD) or RTX 4060 (NVIDIA) — both handle 1080p at high settings with ease. Going lower (RX 6600, RTX 3060) is viable if you find a good used deal. Avoid GPUs older than 3 generations — driver support and VRAM become limiting factors.

CPU — Match your GPU tier
Processor

Don’t pair a weak CPU with a strong GPU — it creates a bottleneck. For a mid-range GPU, the AMD Ryzen 5 7600 or Intel Core i5-13400F are the best value options. Both handle any modern game comfortably. Avoid the highest-end CPUs — gaming performance doesn’t scale significantly above 6–8 cores at 1080p.

RAM — 16GB minimum, 32GB if on sale
Memory

16GB DDR4 or DDR5 is the minimum for gaming in 2026. Go for a dual-channel kit (2×8GB) rather than a single 16GB stick — dual-channel gives a free 5–15% performance boost. Speed above DDR4-3200 or DDR5-5200 has diminishing returns for gaming. Don’t pay a premium for RGB RAM — the LEDs contribute nothing to performance.

Storage — SSD required, NVMe preferred
Storage

Get at least a 500GB NVMe SSD for your OS and main games. HDDs are acceptable for secondary game storage only — never as the boot drive. The difference between a SATA SSD and a budget NVMe SSD is minor in games; both are dramatically better than an HDD. Budget NVMe drives (Kingston NV3, WD Green SN350) cost the same as SATA and perform better.

Motherboard — Compatible, not premium
Motherboard

The motherboard’s job is compatibility. For AMD Ryzen 7000, get a B650 board. For Intel 13th/14th gen, get a B760 board. You don’t need Z-series unless you plan to overclock, which isn’t worth the complexity on a budget build. Check that the board has the M.2 slots you need for your NVMe SSD and USB ports you’ll actually use.

PSU — Don’t cheap out here
Power Supply

A bad PSU can damage your entire build. Stick to reputable brands: Corsair, Seasonic, EVGA, be quiet!. Get at least 80 Plus Bronze certification. For a mid-range build (RTX 4060 + Ryzen 5), a 650W PSU is sufficient. Modular or semi-modular PSUs make cable management easier — worth the small price difference.

Case — Airflow over aesthetics
PC Case

A good case improves thermals, which extends component life. Look for mesh front panels (not solid) for intake airflow. The Fractal Design Focus 2 and Lian Li Lancool 205 are popular budget options with excellent airflow. Avoid cases with solid metal fronts — they restrict intake and raise temperatures.

💡 Use PCPartPicker Before You Buy Anything PCPartPicker.com checks compatibility between all your chosen parts automatically — CPU socket vs motherboard, PSU wattage vs total system draw, RAM compatibility, case clearance. It also tracks prices across multiple retailers. Use it to build your parts list before spending a cent.

New vs Used: When to Buy Used Parts

Used parts can stretch your budget significantly if you buy smart:

  • GPU: Good used buys — especially previous-gen cards (RTX 3060, RX 6700 XT). Check for mining wear.
  • CPU: Safe to buy used — CPUs rarely fail if handled correctly.
  • RAM: Fine used. DDR4 is cheap new too — compare prices before committing.
  • PSU: Avoid used PSUs — they degrade over time and a failure can kill your whole build.
  • Motherboard: Risky used — hard to test without full build. Buy new if budget allows.

Sample Budget Build: ~$600

  • GPU: RX 7600 — ~$250
  • CPU: Ryzen 5 7600 — ~$180 (includes cooler)
  • RAM: 16GB DDR5-5200 (2×8GB) — ~$45
  • Storage: 500GB NVMe SSD — ~$40
  • Motherboard: B650 (budget) — ~$90
  • PSU: 650W 80+ Bronze — ~$60
  • Case: Budget mesh case — ~$50

This build handles 1080p gaming at high-ultra settings in virtually every title in 2026. Add an SSD upgrade or more RAM later — the platform supports it. Bottlenecking: What It Actually Means

A bottleneck occurs when one component limits the potential of another. The most common gaming bottleneck is a weak CPU limiting a strong GPU — the CPU can't feed frame data to the GPU fast enough, so the GPU sits partially idle and you don't get the FPS you paid for.

Use a bottleneck calculator (search "PC bottleneck calculator") to check your planned build at your target resolution. At 1080p, CPU bottlenecks matter more because the GPU finishes frames quickly and waits for the CPU. At 1440p and 4K, the GPU does much more work per frame, so a slightly weaker CPU is less of a problem. Pairing an RX 7600 with a Ryzen 5 7600 at 1080p is well-matched — the CPU won't hold back the GPU in most games.

Where to Buy and When to Buy

Timing your purchase can save you significant money:

GPU Pricing Cycles

GPU prices follow a generation cycle. Prices are highest at launch and drop 15–30% within 3–6 months as supply catches up. The best time to buy a GPU is either right at launch (if stock is available) or 6–12 months after launch when prices have normalized. The worst time is during the first 2 months of a new generation release — you pay a premium for the new card or get stuck with inflated prices on the previous gen.

Where to Find Deals

  • PCPartPicker price history: Shows whether current prices are low or high relative to the past 12 months
  • r/buildapcsales (Reddit): Community-curated deals, posted as they happen
  • eBay for used parts: CPUs and RAM especially — these hold up well used. Verify the seller's feedback score and check for return policies.
  • Amazon Warehouse Deals: Open-box items at 10–30% discount. Check the condition notes carefully.
  • Local marketplace (Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist): GPU deals especially — many upgrade enthusiasts sell their previous card when a new one launches.
Pro Tip: Check GPU VRAM Before Buying VRAM (video memory) is increasingly important in 2026. Games like Alan Wake 2 and Starfield can use 8GB+ of VRAM at high settings. The RTX 4060 has 8GB, which is the minimum for high-setting 1080p gaming. If your budget allows, the RTX 4060 Ti with 16GB provides significantly more headroom. Never buy a GPU with less than 8GB for a new build in 2026.

The Monitor and Peripherals Trap

A common budget-building mistake is spending everything on the PC build and then using a 1080p 60Hz monitor from 2015. Your GPU is doing work it doesn't need to at 60Hz. A 1080p 144Hz monitor pairs much better with a budget gaming GPU than a 60Hz panel — the GPU can actually display the higher framerates it's producing.

Check out the best budget gaming monitors under $200 for displays that match a $600 build well. For peripherals, you don't need to spend much — the best gaming mice under $30 perform as well as mice three times the price for most players.

After Your Build: First Steps

Once the build is assembled and Windows is installed, do these things immediately:

  1. Enable XMP/EXPO in BIOS — your RAM is running at slower-than-rated speed until you do this
  2. Install the latest GPU drivers — from NVIDIA or AMD's website, not Windows Update
  3. Update Windows — critical security patches and driver updates
  4. Run a stability test — 30 minutes of Cinebench R23 for CPU, 30 minutes of Unigine Superposition for GPU. Any crashes indicate a component problem.
  5. Monitor temperatures — install MSI Afterburner and check your CPU and GPU temps during the stress test. See our guide on monitoring PC temperatures for normal ranges.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Buying a cheap power supply: A failed PSU can damage every other component in your system. Stick to well-reviewed brands: Corsair, Seasonic, EVGA, be quiet!. A bad PSU is the most expensive "savings" you can make.
  • Buying parts without checking PCPartPicker compatibility: CPU socket must match motherboard socket. RAM type must match (DDR4 vs DDR5). GPU power connectors must be present on the PSU. Skipping compatibility checks leads to costly returns.
  • Overpaying for an RGB case: A $120 RGB case performs identically to a $50 mesh case for airflow and temperatures. Spend that $70 difference on a better GPU.
  • Ignoring the cooler: Budget motherboard + budget CPU + stock cooler is fine for Ryzen 5 7600 (which includes a Wraith Stealth cooler). But if your CPU doesn't include a cooler, a budget aftermarket option (~$30) is mandatory, not optional.
  • Buying the wrong storage: In 2026, there is no reason to install Windows on an HDD. Budget NVMe SSDs are cheap enough that an HDD-only build is a false economy. The system will feel sluggish every single day.

Advanced Tips

Consider AM5 Platform for Longevity

AMD's AM5 socket (for Ryzen 7000 and beyond) has an extended lifespan — AMD has committed to supporting it through at least 2027, meaning future CPU upgrades are possible without buying a new motherboard. Intel changes sockets more frequently. For budget builders who plan to upgrade incrementally, AM5 offers better long-term value than Intel platforms.

Set BIOS Correctly First Boot

After your first successful boot, immediately enter BIOS and: enable XMP/EXPO for RAM, set your M.2 SSD as the boot drive (if it isn't already), verify all fans are detected, and enable Re-Size BAR / Smart Access Memory if your GPU supports it. Re-Size BAR allows the CPU to access the full GPU VRAM at once instead of in small chunks, providing 2–10% FPS improvement in many games.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy a pre-built or build myself?

Building yourself typically gives you 15–25% more performance for the same money compared to pre-builts at the same price point, because pre-built manufacturers include labor costs and often use lower-quality PSUs and cooling. Building is easier than it looks — most builds take 2–4 hours for a first-timer following a guide. That said, pre-builts have improved significantly in value, and brands like Lenovo Legion and HP Omen offer competitive deals during sales.

How long will a $600 budget build last?

A well-chosen $600 build in 2026 should handle 1080p gaming at high-ultra settings for 3–4 years without needing upgrades. The GPU is typically the first component to feel aging pressure as games become more demanding. When the time comes, upgrading just the GPU (while keeping everything else) is cost-effective and extends the build's life by another 2–3 years.

Is integrated graphics good enough for gaming?

AMD's Ryzen 7000 processors include RDNA 2 integrated graphics that can handle light gaming (indie games, older titles) at 1080p low settings. They're not suitable for demanding modern games. Intel's integrated graphics are weaker. If you're on a tight budget and need to phase purchases, buying a CPU with integrated graphics first and adding a dedicated GPU later is a viable strategy — just know the gaming experience with only integrated graphics will be very limited.

The Golden Rule

Put the most money into your GPU. Match it with a CPU that won’t bottleneck it. Use PCPartPicker to verify everything is compatible before buying. Don’t overspend on RAM speed, RGB, or a premium motherboard — those dollars are better spent on the parts that actually move the frame counter.

A well-planned $600 build will outperform a poorly-planned $900 build. The research is free — do it before you spend.
