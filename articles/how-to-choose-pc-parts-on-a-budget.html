How to Choose PC Parts on a Budget

By Fernando Oliveira May 2026 7 min read Tech Guide

Building a gaming PC on a budget isn’t about buying the cheapest parts — it’s about knowing where to spend and where to save. Spend wrong and you end up with a bottlenecked build that underperforms. Spend right and you get 80% of the performance for 50% of the price.

Budget Allocation: Where Your Money Should Go

Most people building on a budget make the same mistake: they spread money evenly across all parts. Don’t. The GPU and CPU are what determine gaming performance. Everything else supports them.

Part-by-Part Buying Guide

GPU — Spend the most here
Graphics Card

The GPU is the single most important component for gaming. In 2026, the sweet spot for budget gaming is the RX 7600 (AMD) or RTX 4060 (NVIDIA) — both handle 1080p at high settings with ease. Going lower (RX 6600, RTX 3060) is viable if you find a good used deal. Avoid GPUs older than 3 generations — driver support and VRAM become limiting factors.

CPU — Match your GPU tier
Processor

Don’t pair a weak CPU with a strong GPU — it creates a bottleneck. For a mid-range GPU, the AMD Ryzen 5 7600 or Intel Core i5-13400F are the best value options. Both handle any modern game comfortably. Avoid the highest-end CPUs — gaming performance doesn’t scale significantly above 6–8 cores at 1080p.

RAM — 16GB minimum, 32GB if on sale
Memory

16GB DDR4 or DDR5 is the minimum for gaming in 2026. Go for a dual-channel kit (2×8GB) rather than a single 16GB stick — dual-channel gives a free 5–15% performance boost. Speed above DDR4-3200 or DDR5-5200 has diminishing returns for gaming. Don’t pay a premium for RGB RAM — the LEDs contribute nothing to performance.

Storage — SSD required, NVMe preferred
Storage

Get at least a 500GB NVMe SSD for your OS and main games. HDDs are acceptable for secondary game storage only — never as the boot drive. The difference between a SATA SSD and a budget NVMe SSD is minor in games; both are dramatically better than an HDD. Budget NVMe drives (Kingston NV3, WD Green SN350) cost the same as SATA and perform better.

Motherboard — Compatible, not premium
Motherboard

The motherboard’s job is compatibility. For AMD Ryzen 7000, get a B650 board. For Intel 13th/14th gen, get a B760 board. You don’t need Z-series unless you plan to overclock, which isn’t worth the complexity on a budget build. Check that the board has the M.2 slots you need for your NVMe SSD and USB ports you’ll actually use.

PSU — Don’t cheap out here
Power Supply

A bad PSU can damage your entire build. Stick to reputable brands: Corsair, Seasonic, EVGA, be quiet!. Get at least 80 Plus Bronze certification. For a mid-range build (RTX 4060 + Ryzen 5), a 650W PSU is sufficient. Modular or semi-modular PSUs make cable management easier — worth the small price difference.

Case — Airflow over aesthetics
PC Case

A good case improves thermals, which extends component life. Look for mesh front panels (not solid) for intake airflow. The Fractal Design Focus 2 and Lian Li Lancool 205 are popular budget options with excellent airflow. Avoid cases with solid metal fronts — they restrict intake and raise temperatures.

It also tracks prices across multiple retailers. Use it to build your parts list before spending a cent.

New vs Used: When to Buy Used Parts

Used parts can stretch your budget significantly if you buy smart:

  • GPU: Good used buys — especially previous-gen cards (RTX 3060, RX 6700 XT). Check for mining wear.
  • CPU: Safe to buy used — CPUs rarely fail if handled correctly.
  • RAM: Fine used. DDR4 is cheap new too — compare prices before committing.
  • PSU: Avoid used PSUs — they degrade over time and a failure can kill your whole build.
  • Motherboard: Risky used — hard to test without full build. Buy new if budget allows.

Sample Budget Build: ~$600

  • GPU: RX 7600 — ~$250
  • CPU: Ryzen 5 7600 — ~$180 (includes cooler)
  • RAM: 16GB DDR5-5200 (2×8GB) — ~$45
  • Storage: 500GB NVMe SSD — ~$40
  • Motherboard: B650 (budget) — ~$90
  • PSU: 650W 80+ Bronze — ~$60
  • Case: Budget mesh case — ~$50

This build handles 1080p gaming at high-ultra settings in virtually every title in 2026. Add an SSD upgrade or more RAM later — the platform supports it.

The Golden Rule

Put the most money into your GPU. Match it with a CPU that won’t bottleneck it. Use PCPartPicker to verify everything is compatible before buying. Don’t overspend on RAM speed, RGB, or a premium motherboard — those dollars are better spent on the parts that actually move the frame counter.

A well-planned $600 build will outperform a poorly-planned $900 build. The research is free — do it before you spend.
