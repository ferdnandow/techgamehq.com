How to Get Better at FPS Games

By Fernando Oliveira May 2026 8 min read Gaming

Getting better at FPS games isn't just about aim. Game sense, positioning, and the right settings matter just as much. Here's a practical guide to climbing the ranks — no aim bot required.

1. Fix Your Settings First

Step 1

Find Your Ideal Mouse Sensitivity

High sensitivity lets you turn fast but kills precision. Low sensitivity gives accuracy but makes quick turns hard. Most pro players use lower sensitivity than you'd expect — typically 400-800 DPI with low in-game sensitivity.

Start with 800 DPI and an in-game sensitivity that lets you do a 180° turn with one full swipe of your mousepad. Practice with it for two weeks before changing anything.

Step 2

Turn Off Mouse Acceleration

Mouse acceleration means your cursor moves faster the quicker you move the mouse. This makes your movements inconsistent and kills muscle memory. Turn it off in Windows: Settings → Bluetooth & devices → Mouse → Additional mouse settings → Pointer Options → uncheck "Enhance pointer precision".

Step 3

Maximize Your FPS and Refresh Rate

Higher FPS = smoother game = easier to aim. Lower your graphics settings to maximize FPS. Make sure your monitor is running at its maximum refresh rate. Even going from 60Hz to 144Hz makes enemies significantly easier to track.

2. Train Your Aim Deliberately

Aim Training

Use Aim Lab or KovaaK's (Both Free/Cheap)

Aim trainers isolate specific skills and let you practice them efficiently. Spend 15-20 minutes in an aim trainer before your gaming session. Focus on:

  • Flicking — snapping your crosshair to targets quickly
  • Tracking — following moving targets smoothly
  • Micro-adjustments — small corrections on stationary targets

Aim Lab is free on Steam. 20 minutes a day for 30 days will noticeably improve your aim.

💡 Crosshair Placement The single fastest way to improve your aim: always keep your crosshair at head height where enemies will appear. If your crosshair is at the right position before you see an enemy, you barely need to move your mouse to get a headshot. This is called crosshair placement and it's more important than raw aim speed.

3. Improve Your Game Sense

Game Sense

Learn to Use Sound

In competitive FPS games, sound is information. Footsteps, reload sounds, and ability sounds tell you exactly where enemies are. Use headphones (even budget ones), turn up game audio, and actively listen. Many kills come from hearing enemies before seeing them.

Game Sense

Play the Minimap

Check your minimap constantly. Know where your teammates are. Track where enemies have been spotted. Most players ignore the minimap entirely — reading it well puts you in the top 25% immediately.

Game Sense

Watch Your Replays

Every death is information. Watch your death replays and ask: where was the enemy? Why didn't I see them? Was I in a bad position? This is how you identify the specific mistakes you keep making and stop repeating them.

4. Positioning and Habits

Don't Peek When You Can Hold

Holding an angle gives you a massive advantage — you know where to aim and your opponent has to find you. Don't run into open areas when you can wait for enemies to come to you in a position you control.

Stop Rushing

Most lower-ranked players die from rushing into unfavorable situations. Slow down. Check corners. Move with your team. Patience wins more rounds than aggression at most skill levels.

One Game at a Time

Play one game, take a break. Tilting (playing angry after losses) is the fastest way to lose more games. If you lose two ranked games in a row, stop for the day. Come back fresh tomorrow.

The 30-Day Plan

  1. Week 1: Fix your settings, find your sensitivity, turn off mouse acceleration
  2. Week 2: Add 15 min of Aim Lab before each session, focus on crosshair placement
  3. Week 3: Watch one replay after every session, identify your most common mistake
  4. Week 4: Focus specifically on fixing that mistake in every game

Follow this for 30 days and you will rank up. Improvement in FPS games is mostly about deliberate practice, not raw talent.
